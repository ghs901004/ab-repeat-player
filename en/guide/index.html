Practice guide

How to Practice With A/B Repeat

A/B repeat is most useful when a long audio file is too broad for focused practice. Instead of playing the same lesson from the beginning, you mark the exact start and end of a difficult phrase, sentence, or musical passage. Repeating that short section helps you notice details that disappear during normal playback.

Why short loops work

Listening improves when the same sound is heard several times with attention. A short loop keeps the context clear while reducing the amount of new information your brain has to process. For language learners, this is useful for connected speech, weak sounds, intonation, and rhythm. For musicians, it is useful for timing, transitions, and small phrasing choices.

A simple routine

  1. Play the MP3 once at normal speed and identify the part that feels unclear.
  2. Set point A slightly before the difficult sound and point B slightly after it.
  3. Loop the section at 0.75x or 0.5x until you can hear the details.
  4. Return to 1x and repeat the same section without looking at a transcript.
  5. Speak, sing, play, or type along with the loop, depending on your goal.

Choosing playback speed

Slower speed is for analysis, not for staying comfortable forever. Use 0.5x when you need to separate sounds, 0.75x when the phrase is almost clear, 1x for real listening, and 1.25x or faster as a challenge after the section becomes familiar.

Good practice material

The best MP3 files are recordings you have the right to use: your own voice notes, purchased lessons, public podcasts that allow personal listening, interviews, lectures, or music practice files.
